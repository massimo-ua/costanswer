Direct Materials

Direct materials are the raw materials that become part of the product. For example, flour, eggs, water, and salt are the raw materials to make bread.

Data entry fields:

“Name of raw material” is a text field. There are restrictions on this field’s length; please be brief. For now there is not a need for additional technical information so we have not created raw material forms.

“Measurement unit” is a text field. Every raw material must be measured in certain units of length or weight.

Please pay special attention to the “Standard quantity required” field. Depending on the standard quantity approach you chose in “Product/Service settings”, this field corresponds to the standard volume of raw materials required either for “Standard quantity per batch” approach.

The amount of raw materials used during a product’s production has a crucial direct impact on the product cost and is very important for project calculations.

“Price per unit” field is the current average purchasing price during the first month of the project. In other words, you are setting 100% of the price level at the start of the project.

The purchasing cost of the raw material used during the product’s production has a crucial direct impact on the product cost and is very important for project calculations.

Price values must be free of taxes such as sales tax or VAT.

“Seasonal price change” field helps you to model price changes expressed in percent – rise or decline - during the year. When forecast the drop of the purchasing price, you must enter the value with negative.

For example, April is the first month of your project. Its purchasing price is 100%. If you project 2% price growth in May, you enter “2” in the seasonal price change field. That will mean that price will grow 102% compared to April’s 100% price level.

If you think that in June the price level is going to drop 2%, you enter “-2” in the seasonal price change field. That will mean that price will drop down to 98% compared to April’s 100% price level.

“Normal waste” is a loss of quantity of raw material, due to any reason; whether because of its nature of the material itself, the nature of the process, or some other conditions. For example, many materials contain water that tends to evaporate. That leads to reduction of material weight and quantity during the transporting, warehousing, or manufacturing process.

“Cost of raw material beginning” is the value of the particular raw material at the warehouse determined during inventory or according to your accounting reports, and is expressed in average purchasing prices of the previous periods. It does not have direct impact on the product cost.

It refers to the company's current inventory asset valuation and its value should be included in the Cost of Goods Manufactured (COGM) Schedule. This schedule is a very helpful managerial tool. It reports and adjusts the total manufacturing costs for the accounting period (month, year) in the work‐in‐progress inventory account to calculate the cost of goods manufactured.

The amount of COGM is transferred to the finished goods inventory account during the accounting period and is used to calculate cost of goods sold on the Income Statement Report. When comparing the accounting of several Income Statement Reports over time, you can track changes of your gross profit volumes. This helps you set future goals and strategies for sales, inventory, and overhead.

COSTANSWER is using the Weighted Average Periodic Inventory accounting method to compute the value of inventory. It means that the recorded amount of inventory represents a value somewhere between the oldest and newest units purchased, manufactured, or sold. In other words, the cost valuation of the raw material in stock by the end of the month depends on its average monthly purchasing cost.

Important note: Be very careful while choosing the raw material’s measurement unit. When you have big quantity of different materials to be used during the production of one product, you must follow the common denominator of weights or sizes. If you start measuring the first material in liters, it makes no sense to skip to tons when measuring the second material.
